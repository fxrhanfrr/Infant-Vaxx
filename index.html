What are Vaccines?

A vaccine is a biological preparation that helps improve immunity to a particular disease. A vaccine usually contains an agent that resembles a disease-causing microorganism and is mostly made from the weakened or killed forms of the microbe, its toxins or any of its surface proteins. The agent is one that stimulates the body’s immune system to recognise the agent as foreign and destroy it. The agent also needs to remember the foreign agent so that the immune system can identify it more easily and destroy any of the microorganisms that it encounters later.

02

Why are Vaccines important?

During the past years, vaccinations in India have saved more than a billion lives and prevented countless people from falling prey to a wide range of illnesses and disabilities. However, vaccine-preventable diseases such as mumps, measles, and whooping cough are still a threat. They continue affecting children, thus, resulting in hospitalisations and deaths each year. Immunisations are important for two main reasons. First, to protect yourself and second, to protect those around you. Vaccines are one of the best ways to prevent diseases.

However, this puts health at risk of catching some severe or fatal diseases. In reality, taking a vaccination is better and safer than not taking one. Vaccination may not be 100% effective, but it is the best defence against epidemics that kill or disable millions of people.

Transmission: People can contract the virus by ingesting contaminated food or water, or by touching surfaces or objects contaminated with faecal matter and then touching their mouth.
Oral-Oral Route
Less Common: The virus can also be spread through the oral-oral route, although this is less common. This can occur through close contact with an infected person, such as through saliva or respiratory droplets.

Infected Surfaces: The virus can persist on surfaces and in the environment for some time, contributing to its spread in unsanitary conditions.

The DTP vaccine is a combination vaccine that protects against three serious bacterial infections:
Diphtheria: This disease affects the respiratory system and can cause severe throat inflammation, breathing problems, heart failure, and even death.
Tetanus: Also known as lockjaw, tetanus causes muscle stiffness and spasms and can be fatal if untreated.
Pertussis: Commonly known as whooping cough, this disease is characterized by severe coughing fits that can be life-threatening, especially for young children.
The DTP vaccine is typically administered in a series of shots starting in infancy. In the United States, the initial series is given at 2, 4, and 6 months of age, with booster doses given later in childhood and adolescence
Transmission:
 The transmission methods for the diseases covered by the DTP vaccine are as follows:
Diphtheria:
 Transmission: Diphtheria is primarily spread through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. It can also be spread by direct contact with an infected person's sores or contaminated objects.
Incubation Period: Symptoms usually appear 2 to 5 days after exposure.
Tetanus:
 Transmission: Tetanus is not spread from person to person. Instead, it is caused by a toxin produced by the bacterium Clostridium tetani, which typically enters the body through wounds or cuts, particularly if they are contaminated with soil, dust, or animal faeces.
Incubation Period: Symptoms can appear anywhere from a few days to several weeks after exposure to the bacteria.
Pertussis (Whooping Cough):
 Transmission: Pertussis is spread through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. It is highly contagious, especially in the early stages of the illness.
Incubation Period: Symptoms generally start 7 to 10 days after exposure but can range from 4 to 21 days.
Vaccination with the DTP (or DTaP) vaccine significantly reduces the risk of contracting and spreading these diseases.
Who is at risk?
 The risk of contracting diphtheria, tetanus, and pertussis varies based on several factors, including age, vaccination status, and exposure risks. Here's a breakdown of who is particularly at risk for each of these diseases:
Diphtheria:
 Unvaccinated Individuals: People who have not received the DTP (or DTaP) vaccine are at higher risk.
Travelers: Those traveling to regions where diphtheria is more common may be at increased risk.
Close Contacts: Individuals in close contact with someone infected with diphtheria, particularly in areas with outbreaks, are at higher risk.
Tetanus:
 Unvaccinated or Under-vaccinated Individuals: Those who have not received the full series of tetanus vaccinations or have not had recent booster shots are at higher risk.
Individuals with Wounds: People with deep, contaminated wounds or those who have not had proper wound care are at risk, particularly if they have not been vaccinated or if their vaccination status is outdated.
Elderly Individuals: Older adults, especially those who have not kept up with booster shots, are at increased risk.
Pertussis (Whooping Cough):
 Infants and Young Children: Babies and young children who have not completed their full vaccination series are highly vulnerable to pertussis.
Unvaccinated or Under-vaccinated Individuals: Adolescents and adults who are not up-to-date with their vaccinations can contract and spread pertussis.
Pregnant Women: Pregnant women are encouraged to get the Tdap booster during pregnancy to protect both themselves and their newborns, as infants are at high risk for severe pertussis.
Close Contacts: Family members or caregivers of infants, especially those who are not vaccinated, can unknowingly spread pertussis to vulnerable babies.
Overall, maintaining up-to-date vaccinations is crucial for reducing the risk of these diseases and preventing their spread.
Prevention:
 Preventing diphtheria, tetanus, and pertussis primarily involves vaccination and maintaining good hygiene practices. Here’s how each disease can be effectively prevented:
Diphtheria:
Vaccination: The most effective prevention method is receiving the DTP (or DTaP) vaccine, which protects against diphtheria. The initial series is given in childhood with booster shots later.
Hygiene: Practicing good hygiene, such as regular hand washing and covering coughs and sneezes, can help reduce the spread of infections.
Avoid Contact: Avoid close contact with individuals who have diphtheria or are showing symptoms of respiratory infections, especially in areas where outbreaks occur.
Tetanus:
 Vaccination: The tetanus component of the DTP (or DTaP) vaccine is crucial. For continued protection, booster shots are recommended every 10 years, or more frequently if you sustain a high-risk wound.
Wound Care: Properly cleaning and treating wounds, especially deep or contaminated ones, is important. Seek medical attention for serious wounds to assess the need for a tetanus booster.
Hygiene: Ensuring that cuts and punctures are cleaned promptly and thoroughly helps prevent infection.
Pertussis (Whooping Cough):
 Vaccination: The DTP (or DTaP) vaccine for children and Tdap booster for adolescents and adults are effective in preventing pertussis. Pregnant women are also advised to receive the Tdap vaccine during the third trimester to protect their newborns.
Hygiene: Good respiratory hygiene, such as covering coughs and sneezes and frequent hand washing, helps reduce the spread of pertussis.
Avoid Contact: People with symptoms of pertussis should avoid close contact with vulnerable individuals, especially infants, until they are no longer contagious.
General Prevention Tips:
 Up-to-Date Vaccinations: Ensure that vaccinations are up to date for all age groups.
Public Health Measures: Adhering to vaccination recommendations and public health guidelines helps control the spread of these diseases in communities.
By following these preventive measures, individuals and communities can significantly reduce the incidence and spread of diphtheria, tetanus, and pertussis.

Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) is a type of bacteria that can cause serious infections, especially in young children. Before the widespread use of the Hib vaccine, it was a leading cause of bacterial meningitis in children under five years old.
The Hib vaccine, introduced in the 1980s, has dramatically reduced the incidence of Hib infections. It works by stimulating the immune system to recognize and fight the bacteria if exposed in the future. Common infections caused by Hib include:
Meningitis: An infection of the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord.
Pneumonia: An infection of the lungs.
Epiglottitis: An infection of the epiglottis (the flap at the base of the tongue that covers the windpipe).
Septic arthritis: An infection in the joints.
Cellulitis: A skin infection that can spread to deeper tissues.
The Hib vaccine is typically administered to infants starting at 2 months of age, with a series of doses given at intervals to ensure effective protection. Thanks to this vaccination program, cases of severe Hib disease have become quite rare in many parts of the world.
Hib vaccine is usually given in 3 or 4 doses (depending on brand).
Infants will usually get their first dose of Hib vaccine at 2 months of age and will usually complete the series at 12–15 months of age.
Children between 12 months and 5 years of age who have not previously been completely vaccinated against Hib may need 1 or more doses of Hib vaccine.
Children over 5 years old and adults usually do not receive Hib vaccine, but it might be recommended for older children or adults whose spleen is damaged or has been removed, including people with sickle cell disease, before surgery to remove the spleen, or following a bone marrow transplant. Hib vaccine may also be recommended for people 5 through 18 years old with HIV.
Hib vaccine may be given as a stand-alone vaccine, or as part of a combination vaccine (a type of vaccine that combines more than one vaccine together into one shot).
Hib vaccine may be given at the same time as other vaccines.
The Hib vaccine is highly effective and has been instrumental in reducing serious infections caused by Haemophilus influenzae type b. Here are some of the main pros and cons of the vaccine:
Pros :-
 Prevention of Severe Disease: The vaccine is very effective in preventing serious diseases caused by Hib, such as bacterial meningitis, epiglottitis, and pneumonia, which can be life-threatening or cause long-term complications.
Reduction in Incidence: Widespread vaccination has led to a dramatic decrease in the incidence of Hib-related diseases. Before the vaccine, Hib was a leading cause of bacterial meningitis in children under five; now, such cases are quite rare in vaccinated populations.
Community Immunity: By vaccinating a large portion of the population, the spread of Hib bacteria is significantly reduced, helping to protect those who cannot be vaccinated or who are less protected due to other health conditions.
Safety: The Hib vaccine has a strong safety record. Serious side effects are rare, and most reactions are mild, such as redness or swelling at the injection site.
Long-Term Protection: The vaccine provides long-lasting protection, often covering a significant portion of a child’s early years, which is crucial given the age group most affected by Hib.
Cons :
Potential Side Effects: While rare, some individuals may experience side effects such as fever, irritability, or mild allergic reactions. Severe side effects are extremely uncommon.
Vaccine Schedule: The vaccine requires multiple doses over several months, which can be a challenge for some families to adhere to the schedule. However, this multi-dose schedule ensures effective and long-lasting protection.
Cost and Access: In some parts of the world, access to the Hib vaccine may be limited due to cost or supply issues, which can be a barrier to achieving widespread vaccination coverage.
Limited Coverage: The vaccine specifically targets Hib and does not protect against other types of bacterial infections or other strains of Haemophilus influenzae, so other vaccinations and preventive measures are still necessary.
Overall, the benefits of the Hib vaccine in preventing serious, potentially life-threatening diseases far outweigh the potential drawbacks, especially when considering the overall impact on public health and the substantial reduction in disease incidence achieved through vaccination programs.

Rotavirus is a highly contagious virus that primarily causes gastroenteritis, which is inflammation of the stomach and intestines. It is a leading cause of severe diarrhea and dehydration in infants and young children worldwide. Here’s a detailed overview:
Transmission
Mode of Spread: Rotavirus is transmitted via the fecal-oral route. This means it spreads through contaminated hands, surfaces, food, or water.
High-Risk Groups: Children under 5 years old are most at risk, especially in environments with poor sanitation.
Symptoms
 Common Symptoms: Fever, vomiting, diarrhea (often watery and can be severe), abdominal pain, and sometimes dehydration.
Duration: Symptoms usually last between 3 to 8 days. Severe cases can lead to dehydration, which may require hospitalization.
Prevention
 Vaccination: The most effective way to prevent rotavirus infection is through vaccination. The rotavirus vaccine is typically given in a series of doses starting at 2 months of age.
Hygiene: Good handwashing practices and maintaining clean environments can reduce the risk of transmission.
Treatment
 Supportive Care: There is no specific antiviral treatment for rotavirus. Management focuses on maintaining hydration and electrolyte balance. Oral rehydration solutions (ORS) are commonly used to prevent dehydration.
Hospitalization: In severe cases where dehydration is significant, intravenous fluids may be required.
Epidemiology
Global Impact: Rotavirus is a major cause of severe diarrhea in young children worldwide, but vaccination programs have significantly reduced the incidence and severity of the disease in many countries.
Additional Information
 Variants: There are several strains of rotavirus, but the vaccines available cover the most common ones.
Seasonality: Rotavirus infections are more common in the winter months in temperate climates, while in tropical areas, infections can occur year-round.
If you’re concerned about rotavirus or need specific advice, especially regarding vaccination schedules or treatment options, it’s best to consult a healthcare provider.
Types of Rotavirus Vaccines
 There are two main types of rotavirus vaccines currently available:
1.RotaTeq (RV5):
 Schedule: Administered in three doses at 2, 4, and 6 months of age.
Type: This is a pentavalent vaccine, meaning it protects against five different rotavirus strains.
Administration: Given orally.
2.Rotarix (RV1):
 Schedule: Administered in two doses at 2 and 4 months of age.
Type: This is a monovalent vaccine, protecting against one rotavirus strain.
Administration: Given orally
Pros of the Rotavirus Vaccine :
 1.Effective Protection:
Prevention of Severe Illness: The vaccine is highly effective in preventing severe rotavirus gastroenteritis, which can lead to dehydration, hospitalization, and even death.
Reduced Hospitalizations: Widespread vaccination has led to a significant decrease in hospitalizations and medical visits for rotavirus-related diarrhea.
2.Reduction in Mortality:
 1. Global Impact: In many regions, especially in low-income countries, the vaccine has drastically reduced the number of deaths due to rotavirus-related complications.
3.Community Immunity:
Herd Immunity: High vaccination coverage contributes to herd immunity, which helps protect unvaccinated individuals and reduces the overall spread of the virus.
4.Safety:
Extensive Research: Modern rotavirus vaccines (RotaTeq and Rotarix) have been shown to be safe and effective through extensive clinical trials and real-world use.
5.Cost-Effectiveness:
Economic Benefits: The vaccine reduces the economic burden on healthcare systems by preventing costly hospitalizations and treatments for severe rotavirus infections.
Cons of the Rotavirus Vaccine:
1.Side Effects:
Mild Reactions: Common side effects include mild fussiness, diarrhea, or a slight fever. These are generally short-lived and not serious.
Rare Serious Side Effects: There is a very small risk of intussusception (a type of bowel obstruction) associated with the vaccine, though this risk is much lower with current vaccines compared to older versions.
2.Cost:
Expense: The vaccine can be expensive, which may be a concern in some regions or for uninsured individuals. However, many health systems and insurance plans cover the cost of vaccinations.
3.Timing and Schedule:
Vaccination Schedule: Completing the vaccine series requires multiple doses at specific ages (2, 4, and 6 months for RotaTeq; 2 and 4 months for Rotarix), which requires adherence to the vaccination schedule.
4.Not 100% Effective:
Breakthrough Infections: While the vaccine significantly reduces the severity and incidence of rotavirus infections, it does not provide 100% protection. Some vaccinated children may still get rotavirus but are less likely to experience severe symptoms.
5.Pre-Existing Conditions:
Health Considerations: Children with certain pre-existing health conditions (e.g., severe immunodeficiency) may not be eligible for the vaccine, requiring additional considerations and alternative preventive measures.

Pneumonia is a serious respiratory infection that inflames the air sacs in one or both lungs. These air sacs may fill with fluid or pus, leading to symptoms like cough with phlegm or pus, fever, chills, and difficulty breathing. Pneumonia can be caused by various organisms, including bacteria, viruses, and fungi.
Key Points About Pneumonia:
1.Causes:
 Bacterial Pneumonia: The most common cause is Streptococcus pneumoniae. Other bacteria like Haemophilus influenzae and Mycoplasma pneumoniae can also cause pneumonia.
Viral Pneumonia: Viruses such as the influenza virus, respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), and SARS-CoV-2 (the virus that causes COVID-19) can cause pneumonia.
Fungal Pneumonia: Fungi from the environment can cause pneumonia, especially in people with weakened immune systems.
2.Symptoms:
 Common symptoms include a persistent cough, chest pain, fever, and difficulty breathing. Some people, particularly older adults and those with compromised immune systems, may have more subtle symptoms like confusion or lower-than-normal body temperature
3.Risk Factors:
 Infants, young children, and elderly people are at higher risk.
Individuals with chronic diseases such as asthma, COPD, heart disease, or those with weakened immune systems are also more vulnerable.
Smoking, malnutrition, and exposure to pollutants can increase the risk.
Diagnosis:
 Pneumonia is usually diagnosed based on a combination of clinical symptoms, physical examination, and imaging tests like chest X-rays. Blood tests, sputum tests, and pulse oximetry might also be used.
Treatment:
 Bacterial Pneumonia: Treated with antibiotics. The choice of antibiotic depends on the type of bacteria causing the infection.
Viral Pneumonia: Often managed with supportive care like rest, fluids, and fever reducers. Antiviral medications may be used in some cases.
Fungal Pneumonia: Treated with antifungal medications.
Severe cases may require hospitalization, oxygen therapy, or even intensive care.
3.Prevention:
 Vaccination: Vaccines are available to prevent pneumonia, particularly those caused by Streptococcus pneumoniae (pneumococcal vaccine) and influenza.
Good Hygiene: Regular handwashing, covering the mouth and nose when coughing or sneezing, and avoiding smoking can reduce the risk.
Healthy Lifestyle: Eating a balanced diet, getting regular exercise, and managing chronic conditions can help strengthen the immune system.
The pneumonia vaccine is designed to protect against pneumococcal disease, which includes pneumonia, meningitis, and bloodstream infections caused by the Streptococcus pneumoniae bacteria.

Schedule:
 Children: Given as a series of four doses, at 2, 4, 6, and 12-15 months of age.
Adults: A single dose is usually recommended, but specific schedules may vary based on individual health conditions.
2. PPSV23 (Pneumovax 23)
Coverage: Protects against 23 types of pneumococcal bacteria.
Who Should Get It:
 Adults: All adults aged 65 and older should receive this vaccine, typically after receiving PCV13.
People with Certain Health Conditions: Those aged 19-64 who have chronic illnesses, are smokers, or have weakened immune systems should also get this vaccine.
Schedule: Usually given as a single dose, but some people may need a second dose after five years, depending on their health condition.

Prevention: Vaccination is the most effective way to prevent pneumococcal diseases, including pneumonia, which can be severe and life-threatening, especially in older adults and those with chronic health conditions.
Reduction of Severity: Even if vaccinated individuals do contract pneumonia, the disease is usually milder and less likely to cause severe complications.

2. Reduction in Severity: If you do get pneumonia, the vaccine can reduce the severity and prevent complications.
3. Protection for Vulnerable Groups: Especially important for young children, older adults, and people with weakened immune systems.
4. Herd Immunity: High vaccination rates can contribute to community-wide protection, reducing the spread of the disease.

 1. Side Effects: Common side effects include soreness at the injection site, mild fever, or fatigue.
2. Not 100% Effective: While effective, it doesn’t guarantee complete protection against all types of pneumonia or all strains of the bacteria.

Measles, mumps, and rubella (MMR) are three contagious viral diseases that were once common in children before the widespread use of vaccines. They are now much less common due to the MMR vaccine, which provides immunity against all three diseases.
Measles
 Symptoms: High fever, cough, runny nose, red eyes, and a characteristic red rash that usually starts on the face and spreads to the rest of the body.
Complications: Pneumonia, encephalitis (brain swelling), and in severe cases, death.
Transmission: Highly contagious, spreading through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes.
Mumps
 Symptoms: Swollen and painful salivary glands (especially the parotid glands, causing "chipmunk cheeks"), fever, headache, muscle aches, and fatigue.
Complications: Orchitis (inflammation of the testicles), oophoritis (inflammation of the ovaries), pancreatitis, and in rare cases, hearing loss.
Transmission: Spread through saliva or mucus from the mouth, nose, or throat, often through coughing, sneezing, or sharing utensils.
Rubella (German Measles)
 Symptoms: Mild fever, rash that starts on the face and spreads, and swollen lymph nodes. Symptoms are often mild in children but can be more serious in adults.
Complications: The most serious concern is congenital rubella syndrome (CRS) if a pregnant woman contracts the virus, leading to birth defects or miscarriage.
Transmission: Spread through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes.
MMR Vaccine:
 The MMR vaccine is a combination vaccine that provides protection against measles, mumps, and rubella. It is typically administered in two doses during childhood.
Effectiveness: The vaccine is highly effective, with the first dose providing about 93% immunity against measles, 78% against mumps, and 97% against rubella. The second dose increases immunity to about 97% for measles and 88% for mumps.
Safety: The vaccine is safe and serious side effects are rare. Common side effects include mild fever, rash, and soreness at the injection site.
Vaccination has significantly reduced the incidence of these diseases, making outbreaks rare in areas with high vaccination coverage.
Here's a breakdown of the pros and cons of the MMR vaccine:
Pros: 1.High Effectiveness:
 The MMR vaccine is highly effective in preventing measles, mumps, and rubella. After two doses, it provides strong protection against all three diseases.
2.Prevents Severe Complications:
 Measles, mumps, and rubella can lead to serious complications, including pneumonia, encephalitis, hearing loss, and birth defects (from rubella). The vaccine greatly reduces the risk of these complications.
3.Herd Immunity:
 High vaccination coverage contributes to herd immunity, protecting those who cannot be vaccinated, such as infants, pregnant women, and people with compromised immune systems.
4.Widespread Acceptance and Use:
 The MMR vaccine has been used for decades and is a standard part of childhood vaccination schedules in many countries. It has a well-established safety profile
5.Reduces Healthcare Costs:
 Preventing outbreaks of these diseases through vaccination reduces the costs associated with treating the diseases and managing outbreaks.
6.Global Public Health Impact:
 Widespread use of the MMR vaccine has led to the near-elimination of these diseases in many parts of the world, saving countless lives.
Cons:
 Mild Side Effects:
1.Common side effects include mild fever, rash, and soreness at the injection site. These are generally short-lived and not serious.
2.Rare Serious Side Effects:
In very rare cases, the vaccine can cause more serious side effects, such as allergic reactions or febrile seizures (convulsions brought on by fever). However, these are extremely uncommon.
3.Vaccine Hesitancy:
Despite overwhelming evidence of its safety, misinformation and fears about the vaccine, often driven by debunked studies or myths, have led some parents to refuse vaccination. This can result in decreased vaccination rates and the return of disease outbreaks.
4.Not 100% Effective:
While highly effective, the MMR vaccine is not 100% foolproof. A small percentage of people may still contract one of these diseases even after vaccination, though the cases are typically less severe.
5.Multiple Doses Required:
To ensure maximum effectiveness, two doses of the vaccine are required, usually administered during early childhood. This requires adherence to the vaccination schedule.
Availability:
 1.Routine Immunization Program:
The MMR vaccine is included in the Universal Immunization Program (UIP) in India. It is provided free of charge in government hospitals, primary health centers, and community health centers across the country.
2.Private Healthcare Sector:
The MMR vaccine is also available in private hospitals, clinics, and through pediatricians. In the private sector, it may be offered as part of a broader vaccination package, and there is usually a charge for the vaccine.
3.Dosage Schedule:

Typhoid fever is a life-threatening infection caused by the bacterium Salmonella Typhi. It is usually spread through contaminated food or water. Once Salmonella Typhi bacteria are ingested, they multiply and spread into the bloodstream.
Urbanization and climate change have the potential to increase the global burden of typhoid. In addition, increasing resistance to antibiotic treatment is making it easier for typhoid to spread in communities that lack access to safe drinking water or adequate sanitation.
Typhoid fever can be treated with antibiotics. Antimicrobial resistance is common with likelihood of more complicated and expensive treatment options required in the most affected regions.
Even when the symptoms go away, people may still be carrying typhoid bacteria, meaning they can spread it to others, through shedding of bacteria in their faeces.
It is important for people being treated for typhoid fever to do the following:
Take prescribed antibiotics for as long as the doctor has prescribed.
Wash their hands with soap and water after using the bathroom and avoid preparing or serving food for other people. This will lower the chance of passing the infection on to someone else.
Have their doctor test to ensure that no Salmonella Typhi bacteria remain in their body.

Tetanus is an infection caused by bacteria called Clostridium tetani (C. tetani), that are found in the environment. Tetanus is an uncommon but very serious disease that requires immediate treatment in a hospital.
Signs and symptoms
 Symptoms typically occur between 3 and 21 days (average 8 days) after someone is exposed to C. tetani.
The first sign is most commonly spasms of the jaw muscles, or "lockjaw."
Other symptoms can include:
 1.Trouble swallowing
2.Sudden, involuntary muscle spams — often in the stomach
3.Painful muscle stiffness all over the body
4.Seizures (jerking or staring)
5.Headache
6.Fever and sweating
7.Changes in blood pressure and heart rate
Complications
 Tetanus can cause serious health problems, including:
1.Laryngospasm (uncontrolled tightening of the vocal cords)
2.Fracture (broken bone)
3.Pulmonary embolism (blockage in lungs caused by a blood clot)
4.Aspiration pneumonia (lung infection from inhaling spit or vomit)
5.Breathing difficulty
Risk factors
 1. Anyone can get tetanus. However, several factors can increase risk for tetanus infection.
2.Vaccination status
3.People who have never received a tetanus vaccine are at increased risk. Similarly, people who are not up to date with their 10-year tetanus booster shots are also at increased risk. 4.Recent wound
5.People with a recent wound are at increased risk for tetanus. This is especially true if the wound is deep or contaminated with dirt. 6.Other risk factors
The following risk factors also increase risk of tetanus:
 1.Being 70 years or older
2.Having diabetes
3.Having an imunocompromising condition
4.Using injection drugs
5.Causes

Seasonal influenza (the flu) is an acute respiratory infection caused by influenza viruses. It is common in all parts of the world. Most people recover without treatment.
Influenza spreads easily between people when they cough or sneeze. Vaccination is the best way to prevent the disease.
Symptoms of influenza include acute onset of fever, cough, sore throat, body aches and fatigue.
Treatment should aim to relieve symptoms. People with the flu should rest and drink plenty of liquids. Most people will recover on their own within a week. Medical care may be needed in severe cases and for people with risk factors.
There are 4 types of influenza viruses, types A, B, C and D. Influenza A and B viruses circulate and cause seasonal epidemics of disease.
🡺Signs and symptoms
 Symptoms of influenza usually begin around 2 days after being infected by someone who has the virus.
Symptoms include:
 1.sudden onset of fever
2.cough (usually dry)
3.headache
4.muscle and joint pain
5.severe malaise (feeling unwell)
6.sore throat
7.runny nose.
